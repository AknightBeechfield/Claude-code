Sublimation on Headwear

All-over print. Vibrant colours. Permanent. Best on polyester substrates.

All-Over Colour

Sublimation printing uses heat and pressure to convert solid dye particles into gas, which bonds permanently with polyester fibres at a molecular level. The result is vibrant, all-over designs that will not crack, peel or fade — because the ink literally becomes part of the fabric. It is the method of choice for photo-realistic prints, complex patterns, camouflage, all-over brand graphics, and edge-to-edge colour on headwear.

The critical requirement: sublimation only works on white or very light polyester fabrics (or polyester-coated substrates). The dye is transparent, so it cannot overprint dark base colours. Beechfield's polyester performance caps and white-fabric styles are ideal candidates. For cotton headwear, look at DTG or heat transfer instead.

How Sublimation Works

From artwork to finished product in three stages

1. Design & Transfer Print

Your artwork is prepared as a high-resolution file (300dpi minimum, CMYK colour mode) and printed in reverse (mirror image) onto specialist sublimation transfer paper using sublimation inks. The print must account for the product's shape — for headwear, this often means printing flat panel templates that will be cut and sewn into the final product. Colour management is critical: sublimation colours shift during the heat transfer process, so ICC profiles specific to your printer, ink and paper combination should be calibrated.

Artwork Stage

2. Heat Press Transfer

The printed transfer paper is placed face-down onto the white polyester fabric and loaded into a heat press. At approximately 200C and firm pressure for 45-60 seconds, the solid dye particles sublimate (convert directly from solid to gas) and penetrate the polyester fibres. As the fabric cools, the gas reverts to solid and locks permanently into the fibre structure. For headwear panels, flat-bed presses are used on pre-cut fabric pieces before assembly. For finished products, specialist cap presses with curved platens are available.

Production Stage

3. Assembly & QC

For all-over sublimated headwear, individual printed panels are cut to pattern and sewn together by the manufacturer. This "cut-and-sew" approach allows edge-to-edge coverage without blank seam lines. For spot sublimation on finished products, the press is applied directly to the target area. Quality checks include colour accuracy against the approved proof, registration across seams, and a wash test to confirm permanence. Sublimated products should show zero ink transfer when rubbed with a white cloth.

Finishing Stage

Sublimation FAQs

Can I sublimate onto cotton headwear?
No. Standard sublimation only works on polyester or polyester-coated substrates. Cotton fibres do not have the molecular structure to bond with sublimation dye — the ink will wash out immediately. Some suppliers offer "sublimation-ready" cotton products with a polyester coating, but results are inferior to pure polyester. For cotton headwear, use DTG, embroidery, screen print or heat transfer instead.
Why can I only sublimate on white or light fabrics?
Sublimation dye is transparent — it has no white ink. The white of the polyester fabric acts as the "white" in your design. On dark fabrics, the transparent dye is invisible or heavily colour-shifted. There is no sublimation equivalent to the white ink base used in DTG or screen printing. If you need vibrant graphics on dark polyester headwear, heat transfer with a white base layer is the best alternative.
How durable is sublimation compared to other methods?
Sublimation is one of the most durable decoration methods available. Because the dye becomes part of the fibre (rather than sitting on top), it cannot crack, peel, flake or delaminate. It is also highly resistant to UV fading and wash-out. Sublimated products can be washed repeatedly at 40C with no degradation. The only limitation is that the polyester itself may pill or wear before the print shows any deterioration.
What is the difference between spot sublimation and cut-and-sew?
Spot sublimation applies a design to a specific area of a finished product — like the front panel of a cap. It is simpler and suited to logo placement. Cut-and-sew sublimation prints the entire fabric before the product is assembled — allowing all-over, edge-to-edge coverage on every panel. Cut-and-sew requires higher minimum quantities (typically 100-300 units) and manufacturer coordination, but delivers a fully custom, premium result that is not achievable with spot application.
What file format and resolution do I need?
Supply artwork at 300dpi minimum in CMYK colour mode. Preferred formats are TIFF (uncompressed) or high-resolution PDF. For cut-and-sew, you will need artwork laid out on flat panel templates provided by the manufacturer — these account for seam allowances and panel curvature. For spot sublimation, a standard print-ready file with bleed is sufficient. Always request a printed colour proof before production, as sublimation colours can shift from on-screen appearance.
