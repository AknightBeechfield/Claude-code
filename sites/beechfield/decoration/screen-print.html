Screen Print on Headwear

Bold, vibrant branding for high-volume orders. Cost-effective at scale.

High Impact at Volume

Screen print delivers bold, vibrant, opaque graphics on headwear — and it scales cost-effectively for large production runs. Each colour in the design requires a separate screen, so the method is most economical for 1 to 4 colour designs at volumes of 100+ units. The thick ink deposit produces vivid, solid colours that pop on both light and dark fabrics.

For headwear, screen print works best on flat, smooth surfaces — structured cap fronts, flat peak brims, and the body panels of bucket hats. Plastisol inks on cotton and polycotton fabrics are the industry standard for opacity and durability. Water-based inks offer a softer hand feel on lighter colours. Always specify ink type and request a strike-off (test print) before committing to a full production run.

How Screen Printing Works

From artwork to finished product in three stages

1. Screen Preparation

Your artwork is separated into individual colour layers — one screen per colour. Each screen is a fine mesh stretched over a frame, coated with a light-sensitive emulsion. Your design is exposed onto the screen using UV light, which hardens the emulsion everywhere except where ink should pass through. The unexposed areas are washed out, leaving a stencil. Screen mesh count (threads per inch) is selected based on ink type and detail level — higher mesh for fine detail, lower mesh for bold coverage.

Artwork Stage

2. Ink Application

The headwear is loaded onto a printing platen or jig that holds it in position. Each colour screen is aligned (registered) to the product. A squeegee pushes ink through the screen mesh onto the fabric — one colour at a time, with flash-curing between colours to prevent smearing. For headwear, specialist cap-printing platens accommodate the product's curved shape. Plastisol inks remain wet until heat-cured, allowing precise multi-colour registration.

Production Stage

3. Curing & Finishing

After all colours are printed, the ink is fully cured using a conveyor tunnel dryer (typically 160C for plastisol) or heat press. Proper curing is non-negotiable — under-cured plastisol will crack and wash off. A stretch test (pulling the printed area) confirms full cure: the ink should stretch with the fabric without cracking. The product is then quality-checked for colour accuracy, registration alignment and print coverage before packing.

Finishing Stage

Technical Specifications

Typical screen print areas for Beechfield headwear

Front Panel (Caps)

Maximum area: 100mm x 60mm. Structured fronts hold flat against the platen for even ink transfer. Best results with 1-4 spot colours; gradients require halftone screens.

Recommended

Peak / Brim

Flat peaks offer an excellent print surface for under-peak branding or all-over brim graphics. Top-of-peak printing also works on flat-brim snapback styles. Maximum area varies by product.

Good

Side Panel (Caps)

Maximum area: 50mm x 40mm. Single-colour prints work best on curved side panels. Multi-colour registration is more challenging on non-flat surfaces.

Moderate

Crown (Bucket Hats)

Maximum area: 80mm x 60mm. The flat crown panels of bucket hats provide a good surface for single or multi-colour prints. Print on individual panels before assembly for best results.

Screen Print FAQs

How many colours can I screen print on a cap?
Most headwear screen printers can handle up to 6 spot colours, though 1-4 colours is the sweet spot for cost-efficiency and print quality. Each additional colour requires a separate screen, adds setup time and increases per-unit cost. For designs with more than 4 colours, consider simulated process printing (using halftone CMYK screens to simulate full colour) or switch to heat transfer or DTG for unlimited colour at no extra cost per colour.
What is the difference between plastisol and water-based ink?
Plastisol ink is a PVC-based ink that sits on top of the fabric and must be heat-cured. It produces bright, opaque prints on any fabric colour and is the industry standard for headwear. Water-based ink absorbs into the fabric fibres, producing a softer hand feel with a more "vintage" look — but it is less opaque on dark fabrics and requires more passes for coverage. Water-based is also a more environmentally friendly option. For most headwear applications, plastisol is recommended for its durability and vibrancy.
What is the minimum order quantity for screen printing?
Screen printing involves setup costs — screen preparation, ink mixing and machine setup — that make it most cost-effective at 100+ units per design. Some printers offer minimums as low as 25-50 units, but the per-unit cost will be significantly higher. For runs under 50 units, DTG or heat transfer are usually more economical. At 500+ units, screen print becomes one of the most cost-effective decoration methods available.
Can I screen print on mesh-back trucker caps?
You can screen print on the solid front panels of trucker caps, but not directly on the mesh. The mesh back does not provide a stable surface for ink adhesion. If you need branding on the back of a trucker cap, consider embroidery on the mesh, or a small transfer or patch on the solid closure strap area. The structured front panels of trucker caps like the B640 are excellent for screen printing.
How durable is screen print on headwear?
Properly cured plastisol screen prints are extremely durable — rated for 50+ washes without cracking, peeling or fading. The key is correct curing: plastisol must reach an internal temperature of 160C throughout the entire ink film. Under-cured prints will fail prematurely. Water-based prints are slightly less durable but still offer excellent longevity when cured properly. For maximum durability, wash inside-out at 30C and avoid tumble-drying on high heat.
