Heat Transfer on Headwear

Full-colour, photo-quality branding. Ideal for complex logos and small runs.

Full-Colour Precision

Heat transfer is the go-to for complex, multi-colour logos and photographic designs on headwear. Unlike embroidery, transfers can reproduce unlimited colours, gradients, fine text and photographic imagery with no additional cost per colour. Digital transfers are printed onto a carrier sheet and then heat-pressed onto the product, fusing the ink with the fabric surface.

Smooth, flat surfaces give the best adhesion and sharpest results. Structured cap fronts, flat peak surfaces, and stable-knit beanies all work well. Avoid heavily textured fabrics like waffle knits or bouclé where the transfer cannot make full contact with the substrate. For performance and outdoor headwear, choose transfers rated for stretch and moisture-wicking fabrics.

How Heat Transfer Works

From artwork to finished product in three stages

1. Artwork & Printing

Your design is supplied as a high-resolution file — ideally vector (AI, EPS) or 300dpi raster (PNG, TIFF) with transparent background. The transfer is printed onto a specialist carrier sheet using digital (CMYK) or screen-printed (spot-colour) methods. Digital transfers suit photo-quality and complex multi-colour designs. Screen-printed transfers suit bold, opaque spot colours at higher volumes. White ink or a white base layer is added for dark-coloured products.

Artwork Stage

2. Heat Press Setup

The headwear is positioned on a cap-specific heat press platen — a curved or flat surface that matches the product shape. Temperature (typically 150-190C), pressure and dwell time are set according to the transfer type and fabric. Correct platen selection is critical for headwear: flat platens work for unstructured fronts, while curved platens suit pre-curved peaks. A silicone pad or Teflon sheet protects the product.

Preparation Stage

3. Application & Peel

The transfer sheet is positioned on the product, the press closes, and heat plus pressure fuse the ink into the fabric. After the set dwell time (typically 8-15 seconds), the press opens and the carrier sheet is peeled away — either hot peel (immediately) or cold peel (after cooling), depending on the transfer type. The result is a smooth, full-colour print with excellent wash durability when applied correctly.

Heat Transfer FAQs

What file format do I need for heat transfers?
Vector files (AI, EPS, PDF) are preferred as they scale without losing quality. For photographic or complex raster designs, supply a minimum 300dpi PNG or TIFF with a transparent background. Avoid JPEG files as compression artefacts reduce print quality. Your transfer supplier will set up the file for printing and can advise on any adjustments needed for optimal reproduction on headwear.
Will heat transfers crack or peel after washing?
Quality digital transfers are rated for 50+ wash cycles when applied correctly. The key factors are correct press temperature, pressure and dwell time. Under-pressing causes poor adhesion. Over-pressing can scorch the fabric or cause the transfer to become brittle. Always follow the transfer manufacturer's application guide and wash products inside-out at 30C for maximum longevity.
Can I apply transfers to polyester performance fabrics?
Yes, but you need low-temperature transfers specifically formulated for polyester (typically applied at 130-150C rather than the standard 160-190C). Standard transfers on polyester can cause dye migration — where the fabric dye bleeds into the transfer, discolouring it. Low-bleed or polyester-rated transfers contain a barrier layer that prevents this. Always test on a sample before production.
How does heat transfer compare to embroidery on cost?
Heat transfer has a lower per-unit cost than embroidery at small volumes because there is no digitising fee and setup is minimal. At higher volumes (500+ units), the per-unit cost advantage narrows because embroidery becomes more efficient at scale with multi-head machines. Transfers are also faster for complex, multi-colour designs that would be expensive to embroider. For simple 1-3 colour logos at volume, embroidery is often more cost-effective.
What is the difference between digital and screen-printed transfers?
Digital transfers are printed using CMYK inkjet technology — ideal for unlimited colours, gradients and photographic imagery. They are cost-effective at low to medium volumes. Screen-printed transfers use spot-colour inks pushed through mesh screens — producing thicker, more opaque, vibrant colours. They are more cost-effective at high volumes (500+ units) and offer better opacity on dark fabrics. Many decorators offer both and can advise on the best option for your project.
